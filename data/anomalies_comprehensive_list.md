# Comprehensive List of Anomalies in Physics, Astrophysics, and Cosmology

**Compiled: 2026-04-01**
**Total anomalies catalogued: 175+**

---

## SECTION 1: COSMOLOGICAL ANOMALIES

### 1. Dark Matter
The observed gravitational effects in galaxies, clusters, and large-scale structure require ~27% of the universe's energy density to be non-luminous, non-baryonic matter. Despite decades of direct detection experiments (XENON, LUX, PandaX), no dark matter particle has been found; all candidates (WIMPs, axions, sterile neutrinos) remain undetected.

### 2. Dark Energy
The accelerating expansion of the universe (discovered 1998 via Type Ia supernovae) requires ~68% of the universe's energy density in a component with negative pressure. No fundamental physics explanation exists for what dark energy actually is; vacuum energy calculations overshoot by ~120 orders of magnitude.

### 3. Cosmological Constant Problem
Quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy density ~10^120 times larger than the observed cosmological constant. This is arguably the worst prediction in all of physics; no known mechanism can cancel contributions so precisely.

### 4. Hubble Tension
Local measurements of the Hubble constant (SH0ES: H0 ~ 73 km/s/Mpc) disagree with CMB-inferred values (Planck: H0 ~ 67.4 km/s/Mpc) at >5 sigma significance. No systematic error has been identified in either method; new physics may be required (early dark energy, new particles, modified gravity).

### 5. S8 Tension (sigma-8 Tension)
Weak lensing surveys (KiDS, DES) measure a lower amplitude of matter fluctuations (S8) than predicted by Planck CMB data. The ~2-3 sigma discrepancy suggests either systematic errors in lensing measurements or new physics suppressing structure growth.

### 6. CMB Cold Spot
A ~10-degree region in the southern sky (in Eridanus) is anomalously cold (~70 microkelvin below average). A supervoid explanation is insufficient to account for the full temperature decrement via the integrated Sachs-Wolfe effect.

### 7. Axis of Evil (CMB Alignment)
The quadrupole and octopole of the CMB are aligned with each other and with the ecliptic plane. In standard LCDM cosmology, these modes should be randomly oriented; the alignment has a probability of <0.1%.

### 8. CMB Hemispherical Asymmetry
The CMB power spectrum shows a statistically significant asymmetry between opposite hemispheres. LCDM predicts statistical isotropy; the ~3 sigma asymmetry has no standard explanation.

### 9. CMB Parity Asymmetry
Odd-multipole CMB modes have more power than even-multipole modes at low multipoles. This parity violation is unexpected in standard inflationary cosmology.

### 10. Lack of Large-Angle CMB Correlations
The two-point angular correlation function of the CMB is anomalously close to zero for angles >60 degrees. Standard inflationary models predict significant correlations at these scales.

### 11. Baryon Asymmetry (Matter-Antimatter Asymmetry)
The universe contains ~10^9 photons per baryon, but essentially zero primordial antimatter. CP violation in the Standard Model is insufficient by many orders of magnitude to produce the observed asymmetry.

### 12. Cosmological Lithium Problem
Big Bang nucleosynthesis predicts 3-4 times more primordial lithium-7 than is observed in old, metal-poor stars. No convincing astrophysical depletion mechanism or nuclear physics correction has resolved this factor-of-3 discrepancy.

### 13. Horizon Problem (residual)
While inflation was proposed to solve this, the horizon problem (why causally disconnected regions of the CMB have the same temperature) requires inflation to have occurred, and the mechanism triggering inflation and ensuring it lasted long enough remains unexplained.

### 14. Flatness Problem (residual)
The universe's spatial curvature is measured to be extremely close to zero (|Omega_k| < 0.001). Inflation explains this but requires fine-tuned initial conditions for the inflaton field itself.

### 15. Initial Conditions of Inflation
What started inflation? What set the inflaton field to the top of its potential? The theory that solves the horizon/flatness problems itself requires unexplained initial conditions.

### 16. Measure Problem in Eternal Inflation
In eternal inflation, every possible outcome occurs infinitely many times, making probabilistic predictions ill-defined. No agreed-upon measure exists for comparing infinite sets of outcomes.

### 17. Trans-Planckian Problem of Inflation
Inflationary cosmology traces quantum fluctuations back to sub-Planckian wavelengths, where known physics breaks down. Predictions depend on unknown trans-Planckian physics, yet appear robust -- which itself is unexplained.

### 18. Cosmic Coincidence Problem
Dark energy and matter densities are comparable today (same order of magnitude), despite scaling differently with expansion. There is no known reason why we live at the special epoch when these densities are comparable.

### 19. The Void Problem / KBC Void
We appear to reside in a ~600 Mpc underdensity (the KBC void), which is in tension with LCDM predictions for the probability of such large voids. Some argue this local underdensity could partially explain the Hubble tension.

### 20. Large Quasar Group (LQG) Structures
The Huge-LQG (~1.2 Gpc) and the Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall (~3 Gpc) exceed the theoretical ~370 Mpc homogeneity scale. LCDM predicts the universe should be homogeneous above this scale; these structures appear to violate this.

### 21. Giant GRB Ring
A ring of 9 gamma-ray bursts spanning ~1.7 Gpc was discovered at z ~ 0.8. Its existence as a coherent structure would violate the cosmological principle.

### 22. Anomalous Bulk Flows
Some measurements indicate that galaxy clusters are moving coherently at ~600-1000 km/s toward a particular direction over volumes of ~300 Mpc. LCDM predicts much smaller bulk flows on these scales.

### 23. El Gordo Cluster
The massive merging cluster ACT-CL J0102-4915 at z = 0.87 has an infall velocity and mass that are extremely unlikely (~6 x 10^-11 probability) in LCDM simulations. Its existence at high redshift challenges structure formation timescales.

### 24. Eridanus Supervoid
A ~1.8 billion light-year void aligned with the CMB Cold Spot. The integrated Sachs-Wolfe effect from such a void is too small by a factor of ~5 to fully explain the Cold Spot temperature decrement.

### 25. Anomalous CMB Lensing Amplitude (A_L)
Planck data prefer a lensing amplitude A_L > 1 at ~2-3 sigma, when it should be exactly 1 in LCDM. This internal inconsistency in Planck data has not been resolved.

### 26. BAO Curvature Tension
Some BAO measurements combined with CMB data prefer slight positive spatial curvature (closed universe) at ~3.4 sigma. This contradicts inflation's prediction of flatness and is in tension with other datasets.

### 27. Age of the Universe vs. Oldest Stars
Some globular cluster age estimates (13.5-14.5 Gyr) are uncomfortably close to or exceed the LCDM age of the universe (13.8 Gyr). Systematic uncertainties in stellar age dating make this tension ambiguous but persistent.

### 28. Missing Baryon Problem (partially resolved)
Until recently, ~30-40% of baryons predicted by BBN were unaccounted for. While FRB dispersion measures and warm-hot intergalactic medium detections have largely resolved this, the distribution of baryons remains imperfectly mapped.

### 29. Primordial Magnetic Fields
Observations suggest intergalactic magnetic fields of ~10^-15 to 10^-16 Gauss exist in cosmic voids. No established mechanism generates coherent magnetic fields on such large scales in standard cosmology.

### 30. Cosmic Dipole Tension
The dipole anisotropy inferred from radio galaxy and quasar number counts appears discordant (2-5x larger) with the CMB kinematic dipole. If confirmed, this violates the cosmological principle.

## SECTION 2: GALACTIC ANOMALIES

### 31. Galaxy Rotation Curves
Spiral galaxy rotation curves remain flat at large radii instead of declining as expected from visible matter. This is the original motivation for dark matter, but the tight correlation with baryonic distribution (BTFR/RAR) is unexplained in LCDM.

### 32. Baryonic Tully-Fisher Relation (BTFR)
Galaxies follow V_flat^4 proportional to M_baryon with remarkably small scatter (~0.1 dex). LCDM requires fine-tuned feedback to reproduce this tight relation; MOND predicts it naturally.

### 33. Radial Acceleration Relation (RAR)
A tight, universal correlation exists between observed gravitational acceleration and that predicted from baryons alone, across all galaxy types. LCDM must invoke precisely tuned galaxy formation processes to reproduce this emergent regularity.

### 34. Missing Satellites Problem
LCDM predicts ~10x more small dark matter halos around Milky Way-type galaxies than observed satellite galaxies. While ultra-faint dwarfs and reionization suppression have reduced the tension, it remains incompletely resolved.

### 35. Too-Big-to-Fail Problem
The most massive predicted dark matter subhalos of the Milky Way are too dense to host any observed satellite galaxy. Baryonic feedback alone struggles to reduce central densities sufficiently for the most massive subhalos.

### 36. Core-Cusp Problem
LCDM N-body simulations predict cuspy (NFW) density profiles in dark matter halos, but many dwarf and low-surface-brightness galaxies show constant-density cores. Baryonic feedback solutions work for some mass ranges but not all.

### 37. Planes of Satellite Galaxies
The Milky Way, Andromeda (M31), and Centaurus A all have satellite galaxies arranged in thin, rotating planes. LCDM simulations produce such configurations in <1% of cases; finding them in all three nearby hosts is highly improbable.

### 38. Satellite Galaxy Phase-Space Correlation
Satellites in planar configurations show coherent orbital motion (co-rotating). LCDM predicts randomly distributed orbital poles, making the observed coherence statistically anomalous.

### 39. Bulgeless Giant Galaxies
Large disk galaxies exist without classical bulges (e.g., NGC 6946, M101). LCDM predicts that hierarchical merging should build bulges in all massive galaxies; their absence requires fine-tuned merger histories.

### 40. Galaxy Morphology at High Redshift
JWST reveals well-ordered disk galaxies at z > 4-6, when hierarchical assembly should produce chaotic, irregular systems. The early appearance of mature morphologies challenges formation timescales.

### 41. Downsizing Problem
The most massive galaxies formed their stars earliest and fastest, opposite to the hierarchical bottom-up prediction of LCDM. Reproducing this "anti-hierarchical" behavior requires complex, tuned feedback prescriptions.

### 42. Angular Momentum Problem
Simulated galaxies historically lose too much angular momentum during formation, producing disks too small compared to observations. While improved feedback models have reduced this tension, the physical mechanism remains debated.

### 43. Missing Baryons in Galaxies
Galaxies contain only ~20% of their expected cosmic baryon fraction. The mechanisms that ejected ~80% of baryons from galactic halos are poorly constrained and require powerful, precisely tuned feedback.

### 44. Diversity of Rotation Curves
Galaxies with the same maximum rotation velocity show a wide diversity of inner rotation curve shapes. LCDM with standard NFW halos predicts uniformity; the observed diversity requires stochastic baryonic processes.

### 45. Ultra-Diffuse Galaxies (UDGs)
Galaxies like Dragonfly 44 have the luminosity of dwarfs but the size of the Milky Way. Some appear dark-matter-dominated (M/L ~ 50), while others like NGC 1052-DF2/DF4 appear to lack dark matter entirely.

### 46. Dark-Matter-Free Galaxies
NGC 1052-DF2 and DF4 appear to have little to no dark matter, with mass-to-light ratios consistent with stars only. In MOND this is also challenging; in LCDM, tidal stripping scenarios are debated.

### 47. Fermi Bubbles
Two giant gamma-ray lobes extend ~25,000 light-years above and below the Milky Way's center. Their origin (AGN jet vs. starburst) and energetics remain debated.

### 48. Galactic Center GeV Excess
An excess of ~1-3 GeV gamma rays from the Milky Way's galactic center was detected by Fermi-LAT. Whether this is dark matter annihilation or unresolved millisecond pulsars remains unresolved.

### 49. High-Velocity Clouds
Clouds of neutral hydrogen moving at velocities incompatible with Galactic rotation populate the Milky Way halo. Their origins, distances, and relationship to the missing satellite problem are debated.

### 50. Magellanic Stream Length
The Magellanic Stream is longer and more massive than tidal models predict. Ram pressure plus tidal models still struggle to reproduce its full extent and leading arm.

### 51. Crater II Galaxy
This extremely diffuse satellite of the Milky Way has an anomalously low velocity dispersion (~2.7 km/s) and large half-light radius. Its properties are difficult to accommodate in standard LCDM subhalo predictions.

### 52. Leo I Velocity Dispersion
The distant Milky Way satellite Leo I has a rising velocity dispersion profile suggesting a central massive black hole (~3 x 10^6 solar masses). If confirmed, this is unexpectedly massive for such a small galaxy.

### 53. Galaxy Spin Alignments
Large-scale correlations in galaxy spin axes have been reported over ~200 Mpc scales. LCDM tidal torque theory predicts only weak, local alignments.

### 54. Lyman-alpha Blob Anomalies
Giant Lyman-alpha blobs (hundreds of kpc) at high redshift have luminosities and sizes that challenge standard models of gas cooling and galaxy formation.

## SECTION 3: STELLAR ANOMALIES

### 55. Tabby's Star (KIC 8462852)
This F-type star shows irregular, aperiodic dimming events of up to 22%, with a long-term secular dimming trend. No natural explanation (comets, dust, etc.) fully accounts for all observed features simultaneously.

### 56. Methuselah Star (HD 140283)
Initial age estimates for this metal-poor subgiant gave ~14.5 Gyr, apparently older than the universe. While revised estimates with better parallaxes give ~13.7 +/- 0.7 Gyr, it remains uncomfortably close to the age of the universe.

### 57. Przybylski's Star (HD 101065)
This Ap star's spectrum contains short-lived actinides (promethium, technetium) and possibly transuranic elements. No known nucleosynthetic process can maintain these elements in a stellar atmosphere.

### 58. Lithium Depletion in Solar-Type Stars
The Sun's lithium abundance is ~100x lower than meteoritic values, and lithium depletion varies widely among solar twins. Standard stellar models do not predict sufficient lithium destruction in solar-type convection zones.

### 59. Solar Abundance Problem
Revised downward solar CNO abundances (Asplund et al. 2009) cause helioseismic sound speed profiles to disagree with solar models. The base of the convection zone and helium abundance are predicted incorrectly.

### 60. Solar Neutrino Problem (historically)
While largely resolved by neutrino oscillations, the precise metallicity-dependent neutrino fluxes still carry information about the unresolved solar abundance problem.

### 61. R-Process Nucleosynthesis Site
The astrophysical site producing ~half the elements heavier than iron via rapid neutron capture remains debated. While neutron star mergers contribute, their rates may be insufficient; collapsars and magneto-rotational supernovae are discussed.

### 62. Anomalous Cepheid Variables
Some Cepheids in dwarf galaxies have periods inconsistent with the standard period-luminosity relation. This creates systematic uncertainties in the cosmic distance ladder and the Hubble tension.

### 63. First Stars (Population III) Non-Detection
No confirmed Population III (zero-metallicity) star has been observed. Either they were all very massive and short-lived, or our search strategies are insufficient.

### 64. Impossibly Massive Stars
Stars like R136a1 (~250 solar masses) exceed the theoretical Eddington-limited mass of ~150 solar masses. The formation mechanism for such stars (mergers vs. accretion) is debated.

### 65. Thorne-Zytkow Objects
Theoretical stars with neutron star cores inside red giant envelopes. One candidate (HV 2112) has been proposed but not confirmed; their existence and frequency remain uncertain.

### 66. Blue Straggler Stars
Stars in globular clusters that appear younger and bluer than the main-sequence turnoff. While binary mass transfer and collisions explain some, the observed frequency and distribution patterns are not fully reproduced.

### 67. FU Orionis Outbursts
Young stellar objects undergo luminosity increases of ~100x lasting decades. The trigger mechanism (disk instability, companion interaction, planet ingestion) is not established.

### 68. Eta Carinae's Great Eruption
In the 1840s, Eta Carinae briefly became the second-brightest star while ejecting ~10-40 solar masses. No standard stellar evolution model predicts such an event for a star that survived.

### 69. Betelgeuse's Great Dimming (2019-2020)
Betelgeuse dimmed by ~1.6 magnitudes over months before recovering. While a surface mass ejection creating a dust cloud is the leading explanation, the trigger for such a massive ejection is poorly understood.

### 70. Stellar Mass Black Hole Mass Gap
A paucity of compact objects between ~2-5 solar masses (between neutron stars and black holes) was predicted but recent LIGO/Virgo detections have found objects in this "gap." The existence or absence of this gap constrains supernova physics.

### 71. Type Ia Supernova Progenitor Problem
Type Ia supernovae are used as standard candles, but whether they arise from single-degenerate or double-degenerate progenitors (or both) is unresolved. This affects their reliability as cosmological distance indicators.

### 72. Supernova Explosion Mechanism
The detailed mechanism by which core-collapse supernovae explode (neutrino-driven vs. magnetorotational vs. acoustic) is still not fully solved in 3D simulations. Many simulations fail to produce robust explosions.

### 73. Pair-Instability Supernova Gap
Stars of 130-250 solar masses should be completely destroyed by pair-instability supernovae, leaving no remnant. The detection of black holes in this "mass gap" (e.g., GW190521) challenges this prediction.

### 74. Anomalous X-ray Pulsars / Magnetars
Magnetars have magnetic fields of ~10^15 Gauss, orders of magnitude above typical neutron stars. The origin and maintenance of such extreme fields is not fully explained by standard dynamo theory.

### 75. r-Process Enhanced Metal-Poor Stars
Some extremely metal-poor stars (e.g., SMSS J031300.36-670839.3) show unusual abundance patterns. The specific nucleosynthetic events enriching the earliest stellar generations are not uniquely identified.

### 76. Stellar Streams Gaps and Perturbations
Gaps and density variations in stellar streams (like GD-1, Pal 5) suggest gravitational perturbations by unseen dark subhalos. The inferred subhalo population is model-dependent and not yet confirmed by direct detection.

### 77. Hypervelocity Stars
Stars ejected from the Galaxy at >500 km/s require specific mechanisms (Hills mechanism, supernova in binaries). Some hypervelocity stars have trajectories inconsistent with Galactic center origin.

## SECTION 4: SOLAR SYSTEM ANOMALIES

### 78. Pioneer Anomaly (resolved)
The Pioneer 10 and 11 spacecraft experienced an anomalous sunward acceleration of ~8.7 x 10^-10 m/s^2. Ultimately attributed to anisotropic thermal radiation pressure, but the resolution required decades of analysis and the anomaly prompted significant theoretical work.

### 79. Flyby Anomaly
Several spacecraft (Galileo, NEAR, Rosetta, Juno) experienced unexpected energy changes (~1-13 mm/s) during Earth gravity assists. No thermal, tidal, or atmospheric drag explanation has been confirmed; the effect appears to correlate with incoming/outgoing asymptotic velocities.

### 80. Anomalous Lunar Recession
The Moon is currently receding at ~3.82 cm/yr, which if constant would place it at Earth's surface ~1.5 Gyr ago. Tidal dissipation must have been lower in the past, but the detailed history is poorly constrained.

### 81. Faint Young Sun Paradox
The Sun was ~30% less luminous 4 Gyr ago, yet Earth and Mars had liquid water. The required greenhouse warming (CO2, CH4, or other mechanisms) and their precise levels remain debated.

### 82. Planet Nine Hypothesis
Clustering of extreme trans-Neptunian object orbits suggests a ~5-10 Earth-mass planet at ~400-800 AU. Despite extensive searches, no Planet Nine has been found; observational bias may explain the clustering.

### 83. Kuiper Belt Cliff
The number density of Kuiper Belt Objects drops sharply beyond ~48 AU. No dynamical mechanism clearly explains this truncation; Planet Nine or a stellar flyby has been proposed.

### 84. Late Heavy Bombardment (debated)
A hypothesized spike in impact rates ~3.9 Gyr ago based on lunar samples. Whether this was a genuine cataclysm or a sampling artifact remains contested; the Nice model provides a mechanism but timing issues persist.

### 85. Mars Hemispheric Dichotomy
Mars's northern hemisphere is ~5 km lower, smoother, and has thinner crust than the southern hemisphere. Whether this arose from a giant impact, mantle convection, or plate tectonics is unresolved.

### 86. Titan's Methane Mystery
Titan's atmospheric methane should be photolytically destroyed in ~10-100 Myr. No confirmed replenishment mechanism (cryovolcanism, subsurface reservoirs) has been established.

### 87. Venus's Atmospheric Superrotation
Venus's atmosphere rotates ~60x faster than the solid planet. The angular momentum source and maintenance mechanism for this superrotation are not fully explained.

### 88. Mercury's Sodium Tail Variability
Mercury's sodium exosphere and tail show rapid, dramatic variability not fully explained by solar wind sputtering models. The relative contributions of various source mechanisms are debated.

### 89. Saturn's Young Rings
Cassini data suggest Saturn's rings may be only 10-100 Myr old, based on their mass and pollution rate. No convincing formation mechanism has been identified for such young rings around a 4.5 Gyr old planet.

### 90. Enceladus's Heat Output
Enceladus radiates ~15 GW from its south polar region, several times more than tidal heating models predict. The energy source for sustained hydrothermal activity is not quantitatively explained.

### 91. Uranus's Extreme Axial Tilt
Uranus is tilted ~98 degrees, likely from a giant impact. However, such an impact should have disrupted its regular satellite system, which orbits in Uranus's equatorial plane.

### 92. Triton's Retrograde Orbit
Neptune's large moon Triton orbits retrograde, indicating capture. The capture mechanism (binary disruption, gas drag) and how Triton's orbit circularized are debated.

### 93. Jupiter's Dilute Core
Juno measurements reveal Jupiter has a "fuzzy" dilute core extending to ~30-50% of its radius. Standard giant planet formation models predict a compact, dense core.

### 94. Oumuamua
The first detected interstellar object showed non-gravitational acceleration without visible outgassing. Its elongated shape, composition, and acceleration mechanism remain debated (hydrogen ice, nitrogen ice, radiation pressure on thin body).

### 95. Interstellar Object Abundance
The detection of Oumuamua and Borisov suggests ~10^15 interstellar objects per cubic parsec, more than predicted by planet formation models. The ejection efficiency required is surprisingly high.

### 96. Earth's Water Origin
Whether Earth's water was delivered by asteroids, comets, or was accreted with the planet remains debated. D/H ratios partially constrain this but do not uniquely identify the source.

### 97. Moon Formation Details
While the giant impact hypothesis is broadly accepted, reproducing the Moon's composition (isotopic similarity to Earth, volatile depletion, small iron core) simultaneously remains challenging.

### 98. Tunguska Event (1908)
A ~12 megaton airburst flattened ~2,000 km^2 of Siberian forest with no impact crater. Whether it was a comet fragment, stony asteroid, or other object is debated due to lack of recovered material.

## SECTION 5: QUANTUM AND FUNDAMENTAL PHYSICS ANOMALIES

### 99. The Measurement Problem
Quantum mechanics does not explain how or why definite measurement outcomes emerge from superposition states. No interpretation (Copenhagen, many-worlds, objective collapse, pilot wave) is experimentally distinguished or universally accepted.

### 100. Fine-Tuning of Physical Constants
At least ~25 fundamental constants appear to require precise values for a life-permitting universe. No dynamical mechanism sets these values; the anthropic principle and multiverse are philosophical rather than predictive.

### 101. Hierarchy Problem
The Higgs boson mass (~125 GeV) is ~10^17 times smaller than the Planck mass. Quantum corrections should drive it to the Planck scale; maintaining its low mass requires extreme fine-tuning (~1 part in 10^34) or new physics (SUSY, compositeness) not yet observed.

### 102. Strong CP Problem
QCD allows a CP-violating theta parameter, but experiments constrain theta < 10^-10. No reason within the Standard Model forces theta to be so small; the Peccei-Quinn axion solution remains undetected.

### 103. Neutrino Mass Origin
Neutrino oscillations prove neutrinos have mass, but the Standard Model originally predicted them massless. Whether masses arise from the seesaw mechanism, Dirac vs. Majorana nature, or other mechanism is unknown.

### 104. Neutrino Mass Hierarchy
Whether neutrino masses follow a normal (m1 < m2 < m3) or inverted hierarchy is undetermined. Current oscillation and cosmological data have not conclusively resolved this.

### 105. Three Generations Problem
Why are there exactly three generations of quarks and leptons? No principle in the Standard Model dictates this number.

### 106. Fermion Mass Hierarchy
Fermion masses span ~12 orders of magnitude (neutrino to top quark) with no discernible pattern. The Yukawa couplings that set these masses are free parameters with no theoretical explanation.

### 107. CKM and PMNS Matrix Origins
The quark and neutrino mixing matrices have specific values with no theoretical derivation. Why mixing angles take their particular values is unexplained.

### 108. Charge Quantization
Electric charge appears in exact integer multiples of e/3. While grand unified theories explain this, none have been confirmed; magnetic monopoles (predicted by GUTs) remain undetected.

### 109. Quantum Gravity
No consistent, experimentally verified theory unifies quantum mechanics and general relativity. String theory, loop quantum gravity, and other approaches remain mathematically incomplete or experimentally untestable.

### 110. Black Hole Information Paradox
Hawking radiation appears to destroy information about what fell into a black hole, violating quantum mechanical unitarity. Recent proposals (islands, replica wormholes) offer formal resolutions but lack physical clarity.

### 111. Firewall Paradox
Maintaining unitarity, monogamy of entanglement, and equivalence principle simultaneously at a black hole horizon appears impossible. Something fundamental must be modified, but what?

### 112. Cosmological Constant Fine-Tuning
The observed dark energy density requires cancellation of vacuum energy contributions to ~120 decimal places. This is distinct from the "old" cosmological constant problem (why it's not huge) -- it's also: why is the tiny residual nonzero at all?

### 113. Muon g-2 Anomaly
The muon's anomalous magnetic moment measured at Fermilab (~4.2 sigma above SM prediction using the dispersive approach) suggests new physics. However, lattice QCD calculations are converging toward the experimental value, creating a "theory tension."

### 114. W Boson Mass Anomaly
The CDF II measurement (2022) of the W boson mass was 7 sigma above the Standard Model prediction. However, ATLAS and LHCb measurements agree with the SM, creating an unresolved experimental discrepancy.

### 115. B-Meson Anomalies
LHCb observed hints of lepton flavor universality violation in B-meson decays (R(K), R(K*)). While recent LHCb updates reduced some tensions, the pattern of anomalies in b -> s l l transitions persists at ~2 sigma levels.

### 116. Proton Radius Puzzle (partially resolved)
Muonic hydrogen spectroscopy (2010) measured a proton charge radius ~4% smaller than electronic hydrogen measurements. While recent electronic hydrogen measurements have converged, the original discrepancy's source remains debated.

### 117. Neutron Lifetime Discrepancy
Beam experiments (~888 s) and bottle experiments (~879 s) for the neutron lifetime disagree by ~4 sigma (~9 seconds). Systematic errors or exotic neutron decay channels (e.g., to dark matter) have been proposed.

### 118. Anomalous Positron Excess
PAMELA, AMS-02 detected a rising positron fraction above ~10 GeV in cosmic rays. While pulsars can explain this, dark matter annihilation remains a viable interpretation.

### 119. Ultra-High-Energy Cosmic Ray Origin
Cosmic rays above the GZK cutoff (~5 x 10^19 eV) should not propagate far due to CMB interactions. Their sources and acceleration mechanisms are not definitively identified.

### 120. GZK Cutoff Sharpness
While the GZK cutoff has been observed (by Auger, TA), the spectrum around it and possible anisotropies differ between experiments. Source composition and maximum energies are debated.

### 121. Koide Formula
The empirical relation (m_e + m_mu + m_tau) / (sqrt(m_e) + sqrt(m_mu) + sqrt(m_tau))^2 = 2/3 holds to <0.01%. No theoretical derivation exists for this numerical coincidence.

### 122. CPT Symmetry Status
CPT invariance is a theorem of local Lorentz-invariant QFT, but whether it holds exactly is an experimental question. Any violation would indicate fundamentally new physics.

### 123. Dark Photons / Hidden Sectors
Several anomalies (muon g-2, positron excess) could be explained by dark photons or hidden sector particles. Despite extensive searches, no dark photon has been detected.

### 124. Proton Spin Crisis
Only ~30% of the proton's spin comes from quark spins. The remainder from gluon spin and orbital angular momentum is measured to be surprisingly large and complex.

## SECTION 6: BLACK HOLE ANOMALIES

### 125. Singularity Problem
General relativity predicts infinite density singularities inside black holes. Singularities indicate the theory's breakdown, but quantum gravity corrections are unknown.

### 126. Black Hole Information Paradox (detailed)
If black holes evaporate completely via Hawking radiation, the final state is thermal and contains no information about the initial state, violating unitarity. The Page curve has been reproduced via island calculations, but the physical mechanism remains unclear.

### 127. Firewall Paradox (AMPS)
The AMPS argument shows that an old black hole cannot have smooth horizon, maximal entanglement between Hawking pairs, and unitarity simultaneously. At least one postulate of semiclassical gravity must fail.

### 128. Intermediate-Mass Black Holes
Black holes of ~100-100,000 solar masses are predicted but only a handful of candidates exist (HLX-1, some GW events). Their formation pathway and why they are so rare is unexplained.

### 129. Supermassive Black Hole Seeds
SMBHs of >10^9 solar masses exist at z > 7 (within 700 Myr of the Big Bang). Standard Eddington-limited accretion from stellar-mass seeds cannot grow them fast enough.

### 130. M-sigma Relation
Supermassive black hole mass correlates tightly with host galaxy bulge velocity dispersion. The physical mechanism connecting pc-scale black holes to kpc-scale galaxies is not established.

### 131. Hawking Radiation Non-Detection
Despite theoretical confidence, Hawking radiation has never been observed. For astrophysical black holes, the temperature (~10^-7 K for stellar mass) is far below CMB temperature, making detection practically impossible.

### 132. Black Hole Thermodynamics Foundation
Black holes have entropy proportional to horizon area (Bekenstein-Hawking). The microscopic degrees of freedom responsible for this entropy are not identified in a model-independent way.

### 133. Penrose Process Efficiency
While the Penrose process for energy extraction from rotating black holes is theoretically established, whether astrophysical jets actually operate via this mechanism (vs. Blandford-Znajek) is debated.

### 134. Quasi-Periodic Eruptions (QPEs)
Repeated X-ray bursts from galactic nuclei (GSN 069, eRO-QPE1) with regular periods of hours. Their origin -- partial tidal disruption, disk instabilities, or orbiting compact objects -- is not established.

## SECTION 7: GRAVITATIONAL ANOMALIES

### 135. MOND Phenomenology
Modified Newtonian Dynamics successfully predicts galaxy rotation curves with a single parameter (a0 ~ 1.2 x 10^-10 m/s^2) but lacks a fully satisfactory relativistic extension and struggles with galaxy clusters.

### 136. External Field Effect (MOND)
MOND predicts that a galaxy's internal dynamics should depend on the external gravitational field it sits in (violating the strong equivalence principle). Tentative observational support exists, challenging dark matter models.

### 137. LIGO/Virgo Mass Gap Events
GW190814 contained a ~2.6 solar mass object in the NS-BH mass gap; GW190521 contained objects in the pair-instability mass gap. These challenge stellar evolution models.

### 138. GW170817 Multimessenger Constraints
The coincident detection of gravitational waves and gamma rays from a neutron star merger constrained the speed of gravity to equal the speed of light to ~10^-15. This eliminated many modified gravity theories in a single observation.

### 139. Gravitational Wave Memory
GR predicts a permanent spacetime displacement after a GW passes (memory effect). This has not yet been directly detected, though pulsar timing arrays may eventually measure it.

### 140. Frame-Dragging Precision
Gravity Probe B measured frame-dragging to ~19% precision, consistent with GR. However, the experimental uncertainties were larger than hoped due to spacecraft systematics, and more precise tests are needed.

### 141. Gravitomagnetic Effects in Binary Pulsars
While GR passes all binary pulsar tests brilliantly, some alternative theories predict subtle deviations in gravitomagnetic precession that are at the edge of current measurement precision.

### 142. Dark Matter-Baryon Coupling
The tight empirical coupling between dark matter and baryonic distributions (RAR, BTFR, central surface density relation) suggests either DM responds to baryons in a fine-tuned way or gravity itself is modified.

### 143. Abell 520 "Train Wreck" Cluster
This merging cluster shows a dark matter concentration coincident with hot gas but offset from galaxies, opposite to the Bullet Cluster. This is difficult to explain in either CDM or MOND frameworks.

### 144. Gravitational Anomalies in Dwarf Galaxies
Some dwarf spheroidal galaxies show M/L ratios of 10-1000, while others (like Crater II) show unexpectedly low velocity dispersions. The diversity of dark matter content among similar dwarfs lacks a unified explanation.

### 145. Equivalence Principle Tests
While the equivalence principle holds to ~10^-15 (MICROSCOPE satellite), some quantum gravity theories predict violations at higher precision. The Nordtvedt effect in lunar laser ranging constrains but doesn't rule out all violations.

### 146. Nanohertz Gravitational Wave Background
NANOGrav, EPTA, PPTA, and InPTA have detected a stochastic GW background. Whether this is from supermassive black hole binaries, cosmic strings, or phase transitions is being investigated; the amplitude may be higher than predicted from SMBH populations alone.

## SECTION 8: OBSERVATIONAL SURPRISES

### 147. Impossibly Early Galaxies (JWST)
JWST discovered massive, mature galaxies at z > 10-16 that formed within 200-400 Myr of the Big Bang. Standard LCDM galaxy formation models struggle to produce such massive systems so quickly.

### 148. Mature Galaxies at High Redshift
JWST reveals galaxies at z ~ 7-10 with established stellar populations, chemical enrichment, and disk morphologies. The speed of galaxy maturation challenges hierarchical assembly models.

### 149. Massive Quenched Galaxies at z > 3
Galaxies that have already stopped forming stars ("red and dead") exist at z > 3-4. Quenching mechanisms must act extremely rapidly and completely in the early universe.

### 150. Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs)
Millisecond-duration radio bursts at cosmological distances with brightness temperatures exceeding 10^35 K. While magnetar models explain some (e.g., FRB 200428 from SGR 1935+2154), the full diversity of FRB properties lacks a unified explanation.

### 151. Repeating vs. Non-Repeating FRBs
Some FRBs repeat (FRB 121102, FRB 180916) and some do not. Whether these are genuinely different source classes or the same sources observed differently is unresolved.

### 152. FRB 121102 Persistent Radio Source
The repeating FRB 121102 is associated with a compact persistent radio source of unknown nature. The environment and emission mechanism of this source are debated.

### 153. Quasar Redshift-Luminosity Outliers
Some quasars in galaxy groups have discordant redshifts relative to their apparent host galaxies (Halton Arp's observations). While largely attributed to chance projections, a few cases remain debated.

### 154. Lyman-alpha Blobs
Giant Lyman-alpha emission nebulae (~100-300 kpc) at z ~ 2-3 have luminosities of ~10^44 erg/s. The power source (obscured AGN, cold accretion, superwind) is not uniquely identified.

### 155. Anomalous Quasar Proximity Zones
Some high-z quasars have proximity zones (ionized HII regions) much smaller than expected for their luminosity. This suggests they turned on very recently (<10^5 yr) or have variable luminosity.

### 156. Missing Ly-alpha Emitters at z > 7
The fraction of galaxies showing Lyman-alpha emission drops sharply above z ~ 7. While attributed to increasing neutral fraction in the IGM, the patchiness and timeline of reionization are debated.

### 157. Ultraluminous X-ray Sources (ULXs)
X-ray sources exceeding the Eddington limit for stellar-mass black holes by 10-100x. Some are explained by neutron stars with extreme magnetic fields or super-Eddington accretion, but the physics is not fully understood.

### 158. Anomalous Gamma-Ray Opacity
The universe appears more transparent to TeV gamma rays than expected from estimated extragalactic background light (EBL). Some observations suggest lower EBL than galaxy counts predict, or new physics (axion-like particles).

### 159. Cosmic Ray Anisotropy
Small-scale (~10 degree) anisotropies in TeV cosmic ray arrival directions were unexpected. The source of these anisotropies, given cosmic ray diffusion, is not well explained.

### 160. 21-cm EDGES Anomaly
The EDGES experiment (2018) reported an absorption signal at z ~ 17 that was 2-3x deeper than possible with standard physics. While instrumental systematics are suspected (SARAS3 non-confirmation), if real, it requires either colder gas (DM-baryon interactions) or brighter radio background.

### 161. Excess Radio Background
ARCADE 2 and LWA measurements show an isotropic radio background ~5-6x brighter than can be accounted for by known radio source populations. The origin of this excess remains unidentified.

### 162. 511 keV Galactic Bulge Emission
The Milky Way's bulge emits ~10^43 photons/s of 511 keV positron annihilation radiation. The positron source (supernovae, pulsars, X-ray binaries, dark matter) is not definitively identified, and the bulge-dominated morphology is puzzling.

### 163. Amaterasu Particle
In 2023, the Telescope Array detected a 244 EeV cosmic ray from an apparently void direction. At this energy, the particle should point back to its source (small magnetic deflection), but no candidate source exists in that direction.

### 164. IceCube Neutrino Excess at PeV Energies
IceCube detects astrophysical neutrinos at PeV energies, but identifying their sources remains difficult. While TXS 0506+056 provided one association, the bulk of the diffuse neutrino flux is unaccounted for.

### 165. Cosmic Birefringence
Analysis of Planck CMB polarization data suggests a ~0.3 degree rotation of the polarization plane (cosmic birefringence) at 2.4 sigma. If confirmed, this would indicate parity-violating new physics (axion-like fields).

## SECTION 9: THERMODYNAMIC AND ENTROPY ANOMALIES

### 166. Arrow of Time
The microscopic laws of physics are time-symmetric (or CPT-symmetric), yet the macroscopic universe has a clear thermodynamic arrow of time. The origin of time-asymmetry is not derivable from known fundamental physics.

### 167. Past Hypothesis / Low-Entropy Initial State
The Big Bang began in an extraordinarily low-entropy state (smooth, uniform). No dynamical explanation exists for why the initial state had such special (low Boltzmann entropy) conditions.

### 168. Boltzmann Brain Problem
In an eternally expanding universe with a cosmological constant, thermal fluctuations will produce "Boltzmann brains" vastly more often than evolved observers. Most measures in eternal inflation predict we should be Boltzmann brains, which we (presumably) are not.

### 169. Black Hole Entropy and Information
The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy S = A/(4 l_P^2) gives enormous entropy values (~10^77 for stellar BH). How this entropy relates to information content and microscopic degrees of freedom is only partially understood (string microstates for extremal BH).

### 170. Gravitational Entropy
Unlike other forms of matter, gravitational systems have highest entropy in clumped states (black holes), opposite to thermodynamic intuition. There is no general, agreed-upon definition of gravitational entropy for arbitrary spacetimes.

### 171. Maximum Entropy of the Observable Universe
Penrose estimates the maximum possible entropy of the observable universe at ~10^124 (if all matter were in one black hole), while the current entropy is ~10^104. Why the universe started at ~10^88 (post-recombination) is unexplained.

## SECTION 10: OTHER UNEXPLAINED PHENOMENA

### 172. Vacuum Catastrophe
Quantum field theory's prediction of vacuum energy density exceeds observation by ~120 orders of magnitude (or ~60 orders if SUSY existed at TeV scale). This is the largest discrepancy between theory and experiment in all of science.

### 173. Magnetic Monopole Non-Detection
Grand unified theories and Dirac's charge quantization argument predict magnetic monopoles. Despite extensive searches (MACRO, IceCube, MoEDAL), none have been found.

### 174. Proton Decay Non-Observation
Grand unified theories predict proton lifetimes of ~10^34-10^36 years. Super-Kamiokande has pushed the limit to >10^34 years, ruling out minimal SU(5) and constraining other GUTs.

### 175. Dark Matter - Dark Energy Coincidence
Dark matter density and dark energy density are within a factor of ~3 of each other today, despite evolving very differently with cosmic expansion. No known physics connects these two sectors.

### 176. The Nature of Time
Whether time is fundamental or emergent, whether a "present moment" has physical meaning, and how quantum mechanics and GR treat time differently remain foundational open questions.

### 177. Quantum Entanglement and Nonlocality
Bell test violations confirm quantum correlations that no local hidden variable theory can reproduce. Whether these imply genuine nonlocality, many worlds, or retrocausality is debated.

### 178. Wigner's Friend and Observer Paradoxes
Extended Wigner's friend scenarios (Frauchiger-Renner, Brukner) show that quantum mechanics gives contradictory predictions when multiple observers are involved. No interpretation resolves all extended scenarios without additional assumptions.

### 179. Dark Energy Equation of State
Whether dark energy is a true cosmological constant (w = -1) or a dynamic field (quintessence, w varies with time) is not settled. DESI BAO results (2024-2025) hint at w evolving over time at ~2-3 sigma.

### 180. Emergent Gravity Proposals
Verlinde's emergent gravity and related entropic approaches predict MOND-like behavior from thermodynamic reasoning. The theoretical framework is incomplete and predictions for cosmological observables are not yet fully developed.

### 181. Unruh Effect Non-Detection
An accelerating observer should detect thermal radiation (Unruh effect), analogous to Hawking radiation. Despite theoretical confidence, no experiment has achieved sufficient acceleration (~10^20 m/s^2) to detect it.

### 182. Anomalous Nuclear Reactions (LENR, historical)
Claims of anomalous heat production in metal-hydrogen systems persist since 1989. While mainstream physics considers these unsubstantiated, some DoE-funded research continues, and the theoretical mechanism (if any) is unknown.

### 183. Ball Lightning
Luminous spherical objects during thunderstorms have been reported for centuries and occasionally filmed. No single physical model (plasma, silicon vapor, microwave cavity) reproduces all observed properties.

### 184. DAMA/LIBRA Annual Modulation
The DAMA/LIBRA experiment observes an annual modulation in scintillation signal at 12.9 sigma over 20+ years, consistent with dark matter wind. However, no other experiment (XENON, LZ, COSINE-100) reproduces the signal with similar or greater sensitivity.

### 185. Gallium Anomaly (Reactor Neutrino Anomaly)
BEST, GALLEX, and SAGE calibration experiments with radioactive sources measure ~20% fewer electron neutrinos than expected. This could indicate eV-scale sterile neutrinos, but is in tension with other oscillation data.

### 186. MiniBooNE Low-Energy Excess
MiniBooNE observed an excess of electron-like events at low energies, consistent with the earlier LSND anomaly. MicroBooNE results do not clearly confirm simple sterile neutrino explanations, leaving the excess unexplained.

### 187. Anomalous Perihelion Precessions (minor bodies)
While Mercury's perihelion precession famously confirmed GR, some analyses of other solar system bodies and spacecraft orbits suggest residual unmodeled accelerations at ~10^-10 m/s^2 levels.

### 188. Quantum Darwinism Completeness
While quantum Darwinism explains the emergence of classical objectivity through environmental encoding, it does not solve the measurement problem itself -- it describes how information proliferates, not why a single outcome is selected.

### 189. Topological Defects Non-Detection
Cosmic strings, domain walls, and textures are predicted by many phase transitions in the early universe. No cosmological topological defect has been confirmed observationally.

### 190. Baryon-to-Photon Ratio Fine Value
The baryon-to-photon ratio eta ~ 6.1 x 10^-10 is precisely measured via BBN and CMB. No baryogenesis model uniquely predicts this specific value from first principles.
